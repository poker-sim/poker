Poker hand rankings

Every five-card poker hand falls into one of ten categories. Here's the complete order, from strongest to weakest, with an example of each.

1

Royal flush

Ace-high straight, all one suit. The best possible hand.

A♠ K♠ Q♠ J♠ T♠
2

Straight flush

Five consecutive cards, all the same suit.

9♥ 8♥ 7♥ 6♥ 5♥
3

Four of a kind

All four cards of one rank, plus any fifth card.

K♠ K♥ K♦ K♣ 4♣
4

Full house

Three of a kind plus a separate pair.

Q♠ Q♥ Q♦ 8♣ 8♥
5

Flush

Five cards of the same suit, not in sequence.

A♣ J♣ 9♣ 6♣ 2♣
6

Straight

Five consecutive cards of mixed suits.

8♠ 7♥ 6♦ 5♣ 4♥
7

Three of a kind

Three cards of one rank, plus two unrelated cards.

7♠ 7♥ 7♦ K♣ 2♠
8

Two pair

Two separate pairs, plus one unrelated card.

J♠ J♥ 5♦ 5♥ 9♣
9

One pair

Two cards of one rank, plus three unrelated cards.

T♠ T♥ A♦ 6♣ 3♠
10

High card

No pair, no sequence, no matching suits. Ranked by the highest card.

A♠ Q♥ 8♦ 5♣ 2♠

How ties are broken

When two players hold the same hand category, the tiebreaker works through the ranks involved in that hand, then any remaining cards (called kickers). Two players with a pair of kings compare their next-highest card; if those match too, the comparison keeps moving down the hand until a difference is found or the pot is split.

A straight or flush is compared by its highest card. A full house or two pair is compared first by the higher of the matched ranks, then the lower. Suits never break ties in standard Hold'em — a flush in hearts is exactly as strong as a flush in spades with the same card ranks.

Why this matters for equity

Knowing the ranking order is the starting point, but it doesn't tell you how often a given hand actually wins at a real table with real opponents and a real board. That's a function of how many ways your hand and the remaining cards can combine — which is exactly what a Monte Carlo simulation calculates by dealing out thousands of possible outcomes.

Pot odds and equity, explained simply

These two numbers are what turn a gut feeling into a decision. Here's what they mean, how they're calculated, and how the calculator uses them to suggest fold, call, or raise.

What equity actually means

Your equity in a hand is your share of the pot if it were dealt out to the river right now, averaged over every way the remaining cards could fall. A hand with 35% equity wins the pot roughly 35 times out of 100 if the hand were replayed to completion. It has nothing to do with how the betting goes — it's purely a function of your cards, the board, and how many opponents you're up against.

Equity isn't something you can count on your fingers for most hands, which is why simulation is useful: dealing the hand out thousands of times and tallying the outcomes gives a far more accurate number than rules of thumb.

What pot odds mean

Pot odds compare the size of a bet you're facing to the size of the pot you'd be winning. If there's $80 in the pot and your opponent bets $20, you're being asked to risk $20 to win $100 total (the $80 already there, plus their $20). That's pot odds of 20-to-100, or a 20% breakeven price — you need to win the hand at least 20% of the time for the call to make money over the long run.

The formula is straightforward:

breakeven equity = amount to call ÷ (pot + amount to call)

Comparing the two

Once you know both numbers, the decision is a comparison:

  • If your equity is higher than the breakeven percentage, calling is profitable in the long run, even though you'll still lose plenty of individual hands.
  • If your equity is lower, calling loses money on average, even if it feels right in the moment.
  • If your equity clears the breakeven number by a wide margin, that's usually a signal you can bet or raise rather than just call, since you're a clear favorite.

Where this gets harder in practice

Two things make this trickier at a real table. First, you don't know your opponents' exact cards, only a range of what they're likely to hold — which is why the calculator lets you simulate against random hands rather than a single known hand. Second, pot odds alone ignore future betting rounds: sometimes a call is correct because of the money you stand to win later (implied odds), even if the immediate numbers look marginal. The calculator's suggestion handles the immediate math; reading the rest of the hand is still on you.

Reference

Poker glossary

Common Texas Hold'em terms in plain language. Search to jump straight to a word.

Blinds
Forced bets posted before cards are dealt — the small blind and big blind — that seed the pot and give everyone a reason to play.
Board
The community cards dealt face-up in the middle of the table, shared by every player.
Buy-in
The amount of money you exchange for chips to sit down at a table or enter a tournament.
Button
The marker showing which player is the dealer for the current hand. It moves one seat clockwise each hand.
Call
Matching the current bet to stay in the hand without raising it further.
Check
Passing the action to the next player without betting, only possible when no bet is currently facing you.
Community cards
See Board.
Draw
A hand that isn't complete yet but could become strong with the right next card, such as a flush draw or straight draw.
Equity
Your share of the pot if the hand were played out to the end many times over — expressed as a percentage chance of winning.
Flop
The first three community cards, dealt together after the first betting round.
Fold
Giving up your hand and any claim to the pot, forfeiting whatever you've already put in.
Hole cards
The two private cards dealt to each player at the start of a hand, visible only to that player.
Implied odds
An adjustment to pot odds that accounts for money you expect to win in later betting rounds if you complete your hand.
Kicker
An unpaired card used to break ties between two otherwise identical hands, such as two players both holding a pair of aces.
Outs
The cards remaining in the deck that would improve your hand to a likely winner.
Pocket pair
Two hole cards of the same rank, such as pocket kings.
Pot odds
The ratio between a bet you're facing and the total size of the pot, used to work out the breakeven equity needed to call profitably.
Preflop
The first betting round, happening before any community cards are dealt.
Range
The full set of hands a player could realistically hold in a given situation, based on their actions so far.
Raise
Increasing the size of the current bet, forcing other players to match the new amount or fold.
River
The fifth and final community card, dealt after the turn.
Showdown
The point at the end of a hand where remaining players reveal their cards to determine the winner.
Stack
The total amount of chips a player currently has in front of them.
Suited
Two hole cards of the same suit, such as the ace and king of spades.
Turn
The fourth community card, dealt after the flop and before the river.
